# Rabbits Knowledge Base — Smallholder Rabbit Farming

## Species Overview

### Meat rabbits (most common)
- Breeds: New Zealand White, Californian, Flemish Giant, Chinchilla, local crosses
- Cycle: ~12–14 weeks from kit (newborn) to market
- Market weight: 2.0–2.5 kg live weight (1.2–1.5 kg dressed)
- FCR target: 3.0–3.5 (good), 4.0+ (investigate). Higher than poultry but rabbits convert forage.
- Breeding age: does ready at 5–6 months (4–4.5 kg), bucks at 6–7 months
- Litter size: 6–10 kits per kindling; 8 average
- Litters per doe per year: 5–7 (with 4-week kit-to-rebreed cycle), realistic smallholder = 4–5
- Mortality target: <10% kit-to-weaning, <5% post-weaning

### Breeder rabbits (does and bucks)
- A productive doe yields 30–50 weaned kits per year
- Buck:doe ratio 1:8 to 1:10 (one buck services 8–10 does)
- Bucks productive 3–4 years; does 2.5–3 years before fertility drops
- Cull does that miss 3 consecutive matings, deliver <5 kits per litter twice in a row, or show recurring infections.

## Lifecycle Phases (meat rabbits)

| Phase | Age | What's happening |
|-------|-----|------------------|
| Kit | Week 1–4 | Nest box, on doe's milk. Eyes open day 10. |
| Weanling | Week 5–8 | Just weaned; vulnerable to digestive upset. Pellet + hay. |
| Grower | Week 9–14 | Fastest growth phase. Highest feed intake. |
| Market-ready | Week 15+ | 2.0–2.5 kg, ready for slaughter. Past this, FCR worsens. |

## Hutch Setup

### Critical parameters
| Parameter | Optimal | Acceptable | Action if outside |
|-----------|---------|------------|-------------------|
| Temperature (°C) | 15–22 | 10–28 | >30°C: heat stress (give frozen bottles); <10°C: insulate, reduce ventilation |
| Humidity | 50–70% | 40–80% | High humidity + heat = respiratory disease risk |
| Floor type | Wire mesh (15×15mm) over solid base | Bamboo slats | Wood-only floors cause sore hocks |
| Hutch size | 0.5 m² for grower, 1 m² for doe + litter | — | Overcrowding causes fighting and disease |
| Ventilation | Good airflow, no direct draft | — | Smell of ammonia = ventilation too low |

### Stocking density
| Type | Per hutch |
|------|-----------|
| Single doe + litter (kits up to 4 weeks) | 1 m² |
| Doe alone | 0.5–0.7 m² |
| Buck | 0.5 m² (always solo — bucks fight) |
| Growers (8+ weeks) | 0.15 m² per rabbit, max 6–8 per group hutch |
| Weanlings (5–7 weeks) | 0.1 m² per rabbit |

NEVER mix mature bucks together (they will fight, often fatally). Adult does can sometimes be group-housed but will fight if a doe in heat is near a buck.

## Feeding Guidelines

### Daily intake by stage
| Stage | Pellets (g/day) | Hay | Greens |
|-------|-----------------|-----|--------|
| Kit (suckling) | — | — | — (on milk) |
| Weanling (week 5–8) | 60–80 | unlimited | introduce slowly, small amounts |
| Grower (week 9–14) | 120–150 | unlimited | 200–300 g greens/day |
| Adult doe (not pregnant) | 100–120 | unlimited | 200 g greens |
| Pregnant doe | 150–180 | unlimited | 250–300 g greens |
| Lactating doe + litter | 200–280 | unlimited | 300–400 g greens |
| Buck | 100–120 | unlimited | 200 g |

### Feed types
- **Commercial pellets**: 16–18% protein, contains all minerals. Use for grower phase (fastest growth).
- **Hay**: Critical for digestion. Provides fiber that prevents GI stasis. Lucerne (alfalfa), grass hay, dried groundnut hay.
- **Greens**: cabbage, kale, carrot tops, spinach, lettuce (small), banana leaves, sweet-potato vines, moringa. Introduce ONE new green at a time over a week.
- **Concentrate (DIY)**: maize, wheat bran, groundnut cake, soybean cake, fish meal, salt, bone meal. Cheaper but inconsistent.

### Forbidden / dangerous foods
- Iceberg lettuce in large amounts (causes diarrhea)
- Onions, garlic, leeks (toxic)
- Avocado, rhubarb, raw beans (toxic)
- Wet/wilted greens (cause bloat)
- Bread, cookies, sweets (cause cecal dysbiosis)
- Cabbage in large amounts (gas; small portions ok)

### Signs of overfeeding pellets
- Soft droppings, wet vent area
- Refusing hay (only eats pellets — bad sign)
- Obesity (back not visible, fat rolls)
- Gut stasis when feed switches

### Signs of underfeeding
- Slow growth (weight below 1.5 kg at week 12)
- Aggressive feeder behavior
- Doe eating own kits (severe protein/calcium deficiency)

## Common Diseases

### Bacterial
- **Pasteurellosis (snuffles)**: most common. Sneezing, white nasal discharge, eye infection. Spreads via contact and aerosol. Treat: enrofloxacin 5–7 days. Cull chronic carriers (shed bacteria for life).
- **Coccidiosis (intestinal)**: bloody/mucousy diarrhea in 4–10 week kits. Treat: sulfaquinoxaline or amprolium 5–7 days. Prevent: clean water, dry hutches, coccidiostat in feed for high-risk farms.
- **Coccidiosis (hepatic / liver form)**: weight loss, pot belly. Confirmed at slaughter (white spots on liver). Same treatment.
- **Enterotoxemia**: sudden death in weanlings, often after antibiotic use or sudden feed change. Treat: probiotics + electrolytes; usually fatal.

### Parasitic
- **Ear mites (Psoroptes cuniculi)**: scratching ears, brown crust inside ear. Treat: ivermectin injection or topical mineral oil.
- **Fur mites (Cheyletiella)**: dandruff-like flakes on back. Ivermectin.
- **Internal worms**: weight loss, dull coat. Deworm every 2–3 months with ivermectin or fenbendazole.

### Environmental
- **Sore hocks (pododermatitis)**: ulcers on bottom of feet. Caused by wire-only floors or wet/dirty bedding. Treat: provide solid resting board (15cm × 25cm wood) in each hutch.
- **Heat stroke**: rabbits don't sweat. >32°C is dangerous. Ears bright red, panting, lying flat. Treat: cool water on ears, frozen bottles, shade, fan.
- **Trichobezoars (hairballs)**: blockage from grooming. Prevent with hay (high fiber). Treat: pineapple juice, simethicone.

### Reproductive
- **Stuck kindling (dystocia)**: doe straining without delivering. Massage abdomen, oxytocin if available. Often fatal.
- **Mastitis**: hard hot mammary gland, doe rejects kits. Antibiotics. Foster kits to another doe if possible.
- **Cannibalism of kits**: stress, first-time doe, low protein. Improve feed, reduce disturbance.

## Vaccination

Rabbits in West Africa typically need:
- **RHD (Rabbit Hemorrhagic Disease) vaccine**: at 8 weeks, then every 6 months. Mandatory in regions with outbreaks.
- **Myxomatosis**: less common in WA but vaccinate if local outbreaks reported.

Most smallholder farms do NOT vaccinate routinely (cost vs. risk). If buying expensive breeding stock, vaccinate.

## Breeding & Kindling

### Breeding management
- Always bring the doe to the buck's hutch (does are territorial; buck performs poorly in unfamiliar hutch).
- Successful mating: buck mounts, falls off backwards or sideways. Repeat after 5 minutes for higher conception.
- Pregnancy test: palpate abdomen at day 10–12 (small grape-sized lumps).
- Gestation: 30–32 days.

### Kindling preparation
- Day 28: place a clean nest box in doe's hutch with shredded paper or hay.
- Doe pulls fur from her belly to line nest 24–48h before kindling.
- Most kindlings happen at night/early morning. Check first thing in the morning.
- Newborn kits: tiny, hairless, eyes closed. Doe nurses 1–2 times per day for 5 minutes only — DO NOT think she's neglecting them.

### Re-breeding schedule (intensive)
- Re-breed doe at week 3–4 after kindling (kits still nursing).
- Wean kits at week 4–5; doe rests 2–7 days then kindle again.
- Yields ~6–7 litters/year. Hard on does — culling required by year 3.

### Re-breeding schedule (semi-intensive, recommended for smallholder)
- Re-breed doe at week 6–8 after kindling.
- Wean at week 5–6.
- Yields ~4–5 litters/year. Healthier does, longer productive life.

## Formulas (use exact arithmetic)

### Kit survival rate
`Survival % = (kits weaned / kits born alive) × 100`

Worked example: doe kindles 9 kits, 7 survive to wean.
`Survival = 7 / 9 × 100 = 78%` — average. Below 70% = problem (cold, disease, low milk).

### Doe productivity
`Annual kits per doe = litters/year × kits per litter × survival rate`

Worked example: 5 litters, 8 kits/litter, 80% survival.
`Annual = 5 × 8 × 0.8 = 32 kits/doe/year`

### Feed Conversion Ratio (rabbits)
`FCR = total feed (kg) / total live weight gain (kg)`

Worked example: 50 grower rabbits, 240 kg pellets used over 8 weeks, average gain 1.6 kg/rabbit.
`Total gain = 50 × 1.6 = 80 kg`
`FCR = 240 / 80 = 3.0` — good.

### Cost per market rabbit
`Cost per rabbit = (cycle feed + chicks/kits + meds + share of doe maintenance) / market rabbits sold`

Doe maintenance is real: a doe eats year-round whether or not she's producing. Allocate her annual feed cost across the kits she produces.

### Days to market
`Days remaining = (target weight - current weight) / current daily gain`

Worked example: 1.4 kg rabbit gaining 30 g/day, target 2.2 kg.
`Days = (2200 - 1400) / 30 = 27 days` — ~4 weeks.

## Quick Diagnostics — symptom to likely cause

### Sudden death
- Heat stroke (most likely if hot weather + poor ventilation).
- Enterotoxemia (digestive crash, often after feed change).
- Predation (snake, dog, mongoose; check for wounds).
- RHD outbreak (multiple deaths same day).

### White nasal discharge, sneezing
- Pasteurellosis (snuffles). Isolate, treat with enrofloxacin. Highly contagious.

### Bloody/mucousy diarrhea (kits, week 4–10)
- Coccidiosis. Treat amprolium or sulfa.
- Bacterial enteritis. Antibiotic + electrolytes.

### Soft / wet droppings
- Too much pellets, not enough hay.
- Sudden feed change.
- Dehydration → re-fed too much pellet.

### Doe rejects kits / cannibalism
- First-time mother (often improves second litter).
- Stress (loud noise, predator scare, frequent inspection).
- Low protein/calcium in feed.
- Mastitis.

### Limping, swollen feet
- Sore hocks. Add solid resting board, dry the hutch.
- Hutch wire mesh too sharp or wrong gauge.

### Slow growth (below 1.5 kg at week 12)
1. Internal parasites — deworm.
2. Coccidiosis (subclinical) — check droppings, treat.
3. Feed protein too low.
4. Overcrowding stress.
5. Genetics (low-quality breeding stock).

### Hair loss / dandruff
- Fur mites. Ivermectin.
- Ringworm (less common). Antifungal cream.
- Stress / fighting (group housing too dense).

### Doe not breeding
- Out of season (does have receptive cycles; if rejects buck 3 times, wait a week).
- Buck infertile (test with another doe).
- Obesity (overfed doe; reduce feed, lose weight, retry).
- Disease (Pasteurella often sterilizes).

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Buying cheap unverified breeding stock**: poor genetics + Pasteurella carriers = chronic snuffles forever. Pay for grade-A bucks and does from established farms.
- **Wire-only flooring**: causes sore hocks within weeks. Always provide a solid resting area.
- **Mixing breeders**: bucks fight (sometimes fatal). Always solo-house bucks.
- **Sudden feed changes**: kills weanlings via enterotoxemia. Transition new feed over 7 days.
- **Skipping hay**: pellets-only causes GI stasis. Hay is non-negotiable.
- **Overfeeding pellets to does**: obese does don't conceive. Restrict feed when not pregnant/nursing.
- **Re-breeding too aggressively**: 6+ litters/year burns out does in 18 months.
- **Ignoring snuffles in one rabbit**: it's contagious. Quarantine immediately or you'll lose the herd.
- **Mass weaning (taking all kits at once)**: doe gets mastitis from sudden milk buildup. Wean gradually over 3 days.
- **Hutches too small**: rabbits get aggressive, fight, develop sores. Calculate space before stocking.

## Seasonal & Market Timing (West Africa)

- **Dry/harmattan season**: respiratory disease risk increases (dust + dryness aggravate snuffles). Provide humidifier or wet sacks.
- **Rainy season**: hutches stay wet, coccidiosis spikes, sore hocks worsen. Raise hutches, add drainage.
- **Demand peaks**: Christmas, New Year, Easter, Eid. Plan grower batches 12 weeks ahead.
- **Lean periods**: post-festive (Jan–Feb). Consider holding stock or processing into smoked rabbit (better margin).
- **Market acceptance**: rabbit meat is a niche in WA but growing. Premium customers (hotels, restaurants, urban middle class) pay 1.5–2× chicken price per kg dressed.

## Decision: When to Sell Growers

Sell when ANY of these:
1. Average weight reaches 2.0–2.5 kg (week 12–14 typically).
2. FCR climbs above 3.5 — feed converting poorly.
3. Hutch overcrowding — split or sell early.
4. Disease outbreak — emergency sell unaffected stock.
5. Festive demand window — sell at peak price even if a week early.

Do NOT sell when:
- Antibiotic withdrawal period (typically 14 days).
- Within 24h of feeding (gut-full = misleading weight).
- Excessive heat (transport stress kills 1–3% en route).

## Decision: When to Cull a Doe

Cull when ANY of these:
1. Misses 3 consecutive successful matings.
2. Litter size drops below 5 for 2 consecutive litters.
3. Kit survival to wean drops below 60% for 2 litters.
4. Chronic snuffles (Pasteurella carrier) — biosecurity risk.
5. Age >3 years AND productivity declining.
6. Mastitis recurring more than once.

Cull bucks when:
1. Refuses to breed for 4+ weeks.
2. Daughters' litter sizes consistently below average (genetic issue).
3. Age >4 years.

## Eden AI — Rabbit Responses
When the farmer's farm is rabbits:
- Use "rabbits", "kits", "doe", "buck", "weanling", "grower", "litter".
- Use "hutch" not "coop" or "pen".
- Reference Pasteurella, coccidiosis, sore hocks, GI stasis as the recurring health issues.
- For mortality questions, ask first about: temperature, hutch hygiene, feed change history, hay availability.
- Distinguish kit-stage (week 1–4, on milk) from weanling (week 5–8, vulnerable) from grower (week 9+).
- Use kg for weights, weeks for age, litters for breeding output.

## Formulas (use exact arithmetic in answers)

### Average Liveweight at Market
`market_weight_kg = sum(grow-out weights at week 16) / count`
Sample 5–10 grow-outs at week 14–16. Target 1.8–2.5 kg liveweight depending on breed and market preference.

### Specific Growth Rate (rabbits)
Less commonly used than for fish, but the same formula applies:
`SGR (% per day) = ((ln(weight_now) - ln(weight_previous)) / days_between) × 100`

Healthy SGR for grow-out rabbits (week 8–14): 1.0–1.8 %/day. Below 0.8% means feed quality, environment, or disease pressure to investigate.

### Kindling rate (THE rabbit-breeding KPI)
`kindlingRate = (kits_weaned_in_period × 365 / period_days) ÷ active_does`

Worked example: 15 does, measured over 90 days. 60 kits weaned in that window.
`rate = (60 × 365 ÷ 90) ÷ 15 = 243.3 ÷ 15 = 16.2 kits/doe/year`
That's below the 20 minimum benchmark — diagnose: doe age, breeding rest period, environment, kit survival.

Healthy ranges:
- >40 kits/doe/year: excellent (intensive commercial)
- 30–40: good (well-run smallholder/commercial)
- 20–30: fair
- <20: poor — investigate

Levers for improving kindling rate:
1. **Doe rest period**: typical re-breed at 10 days post-kindling for tighter cycles. Wait too long → lower year-over-year output. Wait too little → exhausted does, fewer kits per litter.
2. **Doe age**: peak fertility 6 months–2.5 years. Cull does after 3 years or once productivity drops 20%+ from her peak.
3. **Buck:doe ratio**: 1 buck per 8–10 does is the smallholder norm. Above 1:15, fertility drops. Provide rest days for the buck.
4. **Kit survival to weaning**: target 75–85%. Below that, examine doe milk supply (nutrition), nest box hygiene, environment.
5. **Nutrition**: pellets need to be 17–18% protein for breeders, with ad-lib hay; calcium for milking does.

### FCR (meat rabbits)
`FCR = total_feed_kg / total_liveweight_gain_kg`

Worked example: 100 grow-out rabbits, week 5 average 0.7 kg, week 16 average 2.2 kg.
- Total gain = (2.2 − 0.7) × 100 = 150 kg
- Feed used over 11 weeks = 510 kg
- `FCR = 510 / 150 = 3.4`

That's acceptable for a smallholder operation; commercial target is ≤3.0. Lower FCR = lower feed cost per kg of meat sold.

FCR targets:
- Commercial intensive: ≤3.0
- Smallholder commercial: 3.0–3.5
- Backyard / casual: 3.5–4.5
- >4.5: investigate seriously (feed quality, breed, disease eating into surviving stock)

If FCR is high: 90% of the time it's feed quality (low-protein pellets, mouldy hay) or disease (silent coccidia + Pasteurella reduce gain without obvious mortality).

### Days to Market Estimate
For meat rabbits: 12–16 weeks from birth to market (1.8–2.5 kg).
- Local breeds (NZW × native crosses): 14–18 weeks
- Pure breeds (NZW, Californian, Flemish): 12–14 weeks under good management

`weeks_remaining = (target_weight - current_avg_weight) ÷ avg_weekly_gain`

Avg weekly gain in healthy grow-out: 100–180 g/week. Slow weeks (<50 g) = problem to diagnose.

## Vaccination Protocol (East/West/Central Africa)

### Required vaccinations
| Disease | Primary | Booster | Dose | Notes |
|---------|---------|---------|------|-------|
| Myxomatosis | 8 weeks | every 6 months | 0.5 ml SC | Two-week reaction window — separate from naive does |
| RHD (RHDV1/RHDV2) | 10–12 weeks | annual | 0.5 ml SC | Mortality 90%+ without; combine with Myxo where vaccine available |
| Pre-mating booster | — | 2 weeks before mating | as above | Maintain titre during pregnancy |
| Rainy-season booster | — | start of rainy season | as above | Vector pressure peaks |

Do NOT vaccinate:
- Pregnant does in last 2 weeks of gestation
- Sick rabbits (treat first, vaccinate after recovery)
- Kits under 6 weeks (maternal antibodies block immunity)

### Optional / risk-based
- Pasteurella vaccine (Pastormone or autogenous) — only if your herd has chronic snuffles. Most operations cull carriers + improve biosecurity instead.
- Coccidiostat in feed (sulfaquinoxaline, salinomycin) — seasonal, especially during the rainy season.

### Withdrawal periods (before slaughter)
- Antibiotics: 14–21 days depending on drug
- Vaccines: 21 days
- Coccidiostats: 5–7 days

## Common Pitfalls (don't repeat these)

1. **Not separating buck access from does** — does coming into heat get bred immediately. Either schedule strictly or keep buck in a separate cage.
2. **Heat stress in rainy/hot season** — rabbits don't sweat. Above 30°C they stop eating, fertility drops. Provide shade, cool water, frozen plastic bottles in hutch.
3. **Buying from auction** — high disease load. Always quarantine new stock 14+ days minimum.
4. **Concrete floors with no rest mat** — sore hocks (pododermatitis) = chronic, painful, hurts production. Provide a wooden or rubber mat in every cage.
5. **Sudden feed changes** — GI stasis kills rabbits in 24–48h. Always transition feed over 7–10 days.
6. **No hay** — rabbits need 60–70% of dry matter intake from hay. Pellet-only diets cause dental + digestive issues.
7. **Overcrowding nest box** — does crush kits. One nest box per doe, deep enough to bury kits up to neck level for warmth.
8. **Skipping vaccinations to save cost** — one Myxo or RHD outbreak wipes out the herd; vaccine cost is 100x cheaper than restocking.

## Decision: When to Cull a Doe

Cull a doe if any of:
1. Two consecutive litters with <60% survival to weaning
2. Two consecutive missed conceptions (heat detected, mating done, no pregnancy)
3. >3 years old AND productivity dropped >20% from her peak
4. Chronic snuffles (3+ episodes in 6 months)
5. Sore hocks or other chronic conditions affecting welfare
6. Any signs of mastitis that didn't fully resolve

Replace with a daughter (kept from a high-performing line) or buy from a known-clean source — never auction stock.

## Quick Diagnostics — symptom to likely cause

### Rabbit going off feed (most common cause: GI stasis)
1. Most likely: **GI stasis** (hairball, dehydration, sudden feed change). Action: subcutaneous fluids if you can, syringe pineapple juice or simethicone, gentle abdominal massage. Consult vet within 6h.
2. Possible: **dental issues** — check teeth, look for drool. Old does often grow tooth spurs.
3. Possible: **heat stress** — temperature >28°C? Provide cooling immediately.

### Sneezing / nasal discharge
1. Most likely: **Pasteurella (snuffles)** — chronic, hard to fully cure. Isolate; long course of antibiotics. Cull persistent carriers.
2. Possible: dust from poor-quality hay or pellets. Switch source.
3. Possible: ammonia buildup in cage — clean droppings tray.

### Kits found dead in nest
1. Most likely: **chilled kits** — doe didn't pull enough fur, kits got cold. Add fur from another nest or wool, place hot-water-bottle in nest temporarily.
2. Possible: doe rejected litter (first-time doe, stress). Foster to another doe within 24h if possible.
3. Possible: **mastitis** — kits unfed because doe in pain. Examine doe's mammary glands.
4. Possible: **rats** — kits eaten or harassed. Improve nest box security.

### Sudden adult death
1. Most likely: **RHD or Myxomatosis outbreak** — check vaccination records, isolate immediately, contact vet.
2. Possible: **heat stroke** — find cooler housing.
3. Possible: **GI stasis** missed.
4. Possible: **predator stress** — stress alone can kill rabbits.

## Seasonal & Market Timing (West/Central Africa)

- **Demand spikes**: Christmas/New Year (Dec), Easter (Mar/Apr), independence days, weddings.
- **Price drops**: post-Christmas glut, rainy-season high availability of bushmeat alternatives.
- **Rainy season risks**: humidity → respiratory disease + coccidiosis; vector pressure for Myxo. Vaccinate early, dry the hutch.
- **Dry season risks**: heat stress, water access, dust irritation. Cool hutches, maintain water supply.
