# Poultry Knowledge Base — West African Poultry Farming

## Species Overview

### Broilers (meat chickens)
- Most common: Cobb 500, Ross 308, Hubbard
- Cycle: 6–8 weeks from day-old chick to slaughter
- Market weight: 2.0–2.8 kg live weight
- Feed bands: Starter (1–14 days, 22–24% protein), Grower (15–28 days, 20–22%), Finisher (29 days+, 18–20%)
- FCR target: 1.6–1.8 (excellent), 1.9–2.1 (acceptable), >2.2 (investigate)
- Stocking density: 10–12 birds/m² (deep-litter floor); 20–25 birds/m² (cage)
- Mortality target: <5% over the cycle. >8% = systemic problem.

### Layers (egg-producing chickens)
- Most common in West Africa: ISA Brown, Lohmann Brown, Hy-Line Brown, NERA black
- Lifecycle phases: Chick (1–5 weeks) → Grower (6–12 weeks) → Pullet (13–17 weeks) → Pre-lay (18–20 weeks) → Laying (21+ weeks, peaks at 28–35 weeks, productive through 72+)
- Feed bands: Starter (1–6 weeks, 20–22% protein), Grower (7–14 weeks, 16–18%), Developer/Pullet (15–18 weeks, 14–16%), Pre-lay (18–20 weeks, 16–17% + calcium build), Layer mash (21+ weeks, 16–18% + 3.5–4.0% calcium)
- Production peak: 90–95% lay rate (28–35 weeks), drops gradually to 65–75% by 70+ weeks
- Cull or restart: when lay rate drops below 60% and feed cost per egg exceeds market price

### Dual-purpose / native breeds
- Kuroiler, Sasso, Noiler, Funaab Alpha — popular in West Africa for backyard farms
- Slower growth than commercial broilers (~16 weeks to market) but hardier and dual-purpose
- Lower input costs, lower output, better margins for smallholder farms

## Critical Parameters

### Temperature (brooding stage — first 2–3 weeks is everything)
| Age | Optimal °C |
|-----|------------|
| Day 1–3 | 32–35 |
| Day 4–7 | 30–32 |
| Week 2 | 27–29 |
| Week 3 | 24–26 |
| Week 4+ | 18–24 |

**Critical**: chicks under 24°C in week 1 will huddle, get crushed, develop respiratory issues. Use heat lamps, kerosene heaters, or charcoal pots with proper ventilation.

### Ventilation
- Adult birds need ~5 m³/hour of fresh air per bird
- Smell of ammonia in the coop = bedding too wet, ventilation too low — change litter, increase airflow
- Too much ventilation in brooding kills chicks via cold stress

### Lighting (layers)
- Pullets (1–18 weeks): natural daylight only — extending light early triggers premature lay (smaller eggs, shorter productive cycle)
- Pre-lay onwards: 14–16 hours/day light. Add artificial light (LED 60–100 lux) if natural daylight is shorter.
- Sudden light changes cause stress and drops in lay rate. Adjust by 15 min/day max.

## Feeding Guidelines

### Daily feed by age (broiler)
| Week | Feed/bird/day (g) |
|------|-------------------|
| 1 | 25–30 |
| 2 | 60–70 |
| 3 | 100–110 |
| 4 | 130–145 |
| 5 | 165–180 |
| 6 | 195–210 |
| 7 | 220–235 |
| 8 | 230+ |

### Daily feed by age (layer in production)
- Pullet phase: ~70–90 g/bird/day
- Pre-lay: ~100–110 g/bird/day
- Peak lay (28–35 wk): ~115–125 g/bird/day
- Late lay (60+ wk): ~110 g/bird/day (drop slightly to control body weight)

### Signs of overfeeding
- Wet droppings, ammonia smell builds fast
- FCR climbs past 1.9 (broilers) or 2.4 (layers)
- Birds becoming sluggish; legs splay (broilers)

### Signs of underfeeding
- Birds crowd feeders aggressively at feeding time
- Body weight below standard for age
- Layers: drop in lay rate within 7 days

## Common Diseases

### Viral
- **Newcastle Disease (NCD)**: paralysis, twisted necks, sudden mortality. NO TREATMENT. Vaccinate at day 7, day 21, week 8, then every 3 months.
- **Infectious Bronchitis (IB)**: respiratory distress, drop in lay, soft-shelled eggs. Vaccinate alongside NCD.
- **Gumboro (IBD)**: bloody diarrhea, prostration, mortality 2–4 weeks. Vaccinate day 14 and day 21.
- **Avian Influenza (HPAI)**: notifiable; if suspected, contact veterinary authorities immediately. No treatment, mass cull required.

### Bacterial
- **Coccidiosis**: bloody droppings, hunched posture. Treat with amprolium or sulfadimidine 5–7 days. Coccidiostat in feed prevents recurrence.
- **Fowl typhoid (Salmonella gallinarum)**: green diarrhea, sudden mortality. Antibiotics (enrofloxacin) + improved hygiene.
- **CRD/Mycoplasma**: rattling breath, swollen sinuses. Tylosin or tilmicosin in water, 5 days.
- **E. coli**: airsacculitis, mortality after stress events. Antibiotics + reduce stress factors.

### Parasitic
- **Lice/mites**: feather damage, scratching, drop in lay. Pyrethroid spray on birds and coop.
- **Worms (roundworm, tapeworm)**: weight loss, pale combs. Deworm with albendazole or piperazine every 8 weeks.

## Formulas (use exact arithmetic)

### Mortality rate
`Mortality % = (deaths / initial flock size) × 100`

Worked example: started with 500 chicks, lost 18 by week 8.
`Mortality = 18 / 500 × 100 = 3.6%` — within target (<5%).

### Survival rate
`Survival % = (current count / initial count) × 100`

### Daily gain (broilers)
`Daily gain (g) = (current weight - previous weight) / days between checks`

Worked example: week 4 weight 1.0 kg, week 5 weight 1.5 kg.
`Daily gain = (1500 - 1000) / 7 = 71 g/day` — on target for Cobb 500.

### Feed Conversion Ratio (FCR — broilers)
`FCR = total feed consumed (kg) / total live weight produced (kg)`

Worked example: 500 birds, 1,200 kg feed used, average 2.0 kg per bird at slaughter.
`Total weight = 500 × 2.0 = 1000 kg`
`FCR = 1200 / 1000 = 1.2` — excellent (suspicious if too low; typical good is 1.6–1.8).

### Feed Conversion Ratio (layers — kg feed per dozen eggs)
`FCR layer = total feed (kg) / (total eggs / 12)`

Worked example: 100 layers eat 12 kg/day, lay 80 eggs/day.
`Daily FCR = 12 / (80/12) = 12 / 6.67 = 1.8 kg feed per dozen eggs` — acceptable (good: 1.5–1.7).

### Production rate (lay rate)
`Lay rate % = (eggs collected / hens housed) × 100`

Worked example: 400 hens, 360 eggs today.
`Lay rate = 360 / 400 × 100 = 90%` — peak production.

### Cost per egg
`Cost per egg = total expenses (feed + chicks + meds + labor + power) / total eggs`

Use rolling 30 days for stable numbers.

### Cost per bird (broilers)
`Cost per bird = total cycle expenses / birds reaching market`

If cycle cost is 950,000 XAF and 480 birds reach market, cost per bird is 1,979 XAF. If sale price is 2,500 XAF/bird, profit margin is ~21%.

### Days to market (broilers)
`Days remaining = (target weight - current weight) / current daily gain`

Worked example: bird at 1.4 kg, gaining 70 g/day, target 2.2 kg.
`Days = (2200 - 1400) / 70 = 11.4 days`

## Quick Diagnostics — symptom to likely cause

### Sudden mass mortality
1. Disease outbreak (NCD or HPAI most likely if rapid). Check for nervous symptoms.
2. Heat stress (hot afternoon, no airflow). Birds gasping, wings spread.
3. Water cut off. Check water lines.
4. Predation (snake, fox in the coop overnight).
5. Suffocation (overcrowding at corners during cold night).

### Birds gasping with open beaks
1. Heat stress (most likely if ambient >32°C). Add fans, mist, cool water.
2. Respiratory disease (CRD, IB). Listen for rattling breath.
3. Ammonia poisoning (high ammonia from wet litter). Change bedding immediately.

### Drop in lay rate (layers)
1. Stress event (predator scare, sudden noise, vaccination). Recovers in 3–7 days.
2. Disease — IB, EDS, fatty liver. Check egg quality (soft shells, watery whites).
3. Light schedule changed. Verify 14–16 hr/day.
4. Feed switched or feed quality drop. Test protein.
5. Bird age — gradual decline after week 50 is normal.
6. Heat stress.

### Bloody droppings
- Coccidiosis (most likely in 2–6 week chicks). Treat amprolium 5 days.
- Necrotic enteritis (less common, often follows coccidiosis). Antibiotic.
- Internal parasites. Deworm.

### Pale combs and wattles
- Anemia (parasites, blood loss).
- Internal worms.
- Vitamin deficiency.

### Birds piling/huddling
- Cold stress (most likely brooding age). Increase heat.
- Fear (predator/loud noise outside).

### Cannibalism / pecking
- Overcrowding — reduce density.
- Lighting too bright. Use red bulbs or dim.
- Protein deficiency or salt deficiency. Test feed.
- Boredom — add scratch litter, hanging cabbage.

### Cracked or thin-shelled eggs
- Calcium deficiency. Increase oyster shell or limestone in feed.
- Old hens. Cull bottom 20% if persistent.
- Heat stress (afternoon). Cool the coop.
- IB or EDS virus. Vaccinate next batch.

## Brooding Setup Checklist (first 2 weeks decide the cycle)

1. **Pre-warm the brooder 24h before chicks arrive** — temperature stable at 32–35°C.
2. **Litter**: 5–10cm deep wood shavings or rice husks. Dry, clean.
3. **Heat source**: 1 brooder lamp (250W) per 100 chicks. Backup gas/charcoal in case of power cut.
4. **Water**: 2 drinkers per 100 chicks, sugar water (5g/L) for first 2 hours to combat transport stress.
5. **Feed**: chick starter mash spread on paper sheets for first 3 days (chicks find it easier).
6. **Light**: 24 hours for first 3 days, then 23 hours from day 4–7, then natural light.
7. **Density**: 30 chicks/m² for week 1, reduce as they grow.
8. **Daily checks**: count droppings, listen for chirping (loud chirps = cold; silent = ok or sick), watch huddle pattern.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Cheap fake feed**: bags labeled "20% protein" are often 14–16% in unbranded markets. FCR will be terrible. Stick to known brands (Hybrid, ECWA, Top Feeds, Vital, Olam).
- **Skipping vaccinations**: NCD will wipe out a flock in 72 hours. The vaccine costs less than 50 XAF per bird.
- **Mixed-age flocks in same coop**: disease spreads from older to younger. Always all-in / all-out per coop.
- **Buying chicks from unverified hatcheries**: poor genetics, parent stock disease. Pay more for grade-A hatcheries.
- **Watering down feed costs at expense of protein**: never substitute >20% of complete feed with maize/cassava — protein drops, FCR doubles.
- **Late vaccination scheduling**: missing day-7 NCD because "the chicks look fine" — by the time they look unwell, it's too late.
- **Litter not turned**: caked litter = ammonia release = respiratory issues. Turn or top up weekly.
- **Predator gaps**: snakes, rats, civets enter through one missed gap. Inspect coop perimeter weekly.
- **Layers in dim light too long**: pullets force-lit too early lay smaller eggs and burn out by week 50.
- **Selling broilers too early or too late**: each extra day past 6 weeks costs more than the weight gained. Track FCR daily; sell when FCR climbs above 2.0.

## Seasonal & Market Timing (West Africa)

- **Demand peaks**: Christmas/New Year, Easter, Eid, independence days, end-of-month for layers (institutional egg buyers). Plan broiler harvests 6 weeks ahead.
- **Price drops**: post-festive glut (Jan, post-Eid), rainy season market closures.
- **Rainy season risks**: cold-stress on chicks, mud increases coccidiosis. Raise the brooder, dry litter daily.
- **Dry/harmattan season risks**: respiratory disease (dust + dryness), water shortage. Check water cleanliness daily.
- **Chick supply**: prices spike in Nov (everyone gearing up for Christmas). Order 2 months ahead for Christmas-target broilers.

## Stocking Density Rules

| System | Broilers | Layers |
|---|---|---|
| Deep litter | 10–12 birds/m² | 6–8 birds/m² |
| Slatted floor | 14–16 birds/m² | 10–12 birds/m² |
| Battery cage | 22–25 birds/m² (mostly illegal in EU; common in WA) | 14–16 birds/m² |
| Free range | 1–2 birds/m² | 0.5–1 bird/m² |

Higher density requires: better ventilation, more drinker/feeder line, more frequent litter management.

## Decision: When to Sell Broilers

Sell when ANY of these:
1. Average weight reaches market target (2.0–2.5 kg).
2. FCR climbs above 2.0 — feed converting poorly, more days = more cost without gain.
3. Mortality spike (>1% in 48 hours) — cut losses, sell healthy stock.
4. Feed price spiked or sale price peaked.
5. Disease outbreak — partial emergency harvest of unaffected birds.

Do NOT sell when:
- Within antibiotic withdrawal period (typically 5–14 days, check label).
- During heavy rain/cold (transport stress, lower buyer confidence).
- 24h after feeding (gut-full birds weigh more but stress drops live weight before sale).

## Decision: When to Cull Layers

Cull when ANY of these:
1. Lay rate drops below 60% and stays there for 4 weeks.
2. Bird age >75 weeks AND egg shell quality dropping (cracked, soft).
3. Feed cost per egg approaches or exceeds market price.
4. Disease outbreak with permanent damage (chronic CRD).

Best practice: cull bottom 20% of producers monthly (skinny birds, dirty vents, no recent lay) — they eat feed without producing.

## Eden AI — Poultry Responses
When the farmer's farm is poultry:
- Use "flock", "birds", "hens", "chicks", "broilers", "layers" appropriately.
- Reference NCD/IB/Gumboro vaccination by name when relevant.
- For mortality questions, ask first about: temperature in past 24h, vaccination status, water cleanliness, litter condition.
- Distinguish broilers (FCR-focused, short cycle) from layers (lay rate-focused, long cycle).
- Use kg for broiler weights and dozen eggs for layer production.
